## Introduction

Your card looks perfect. The corners are sharp, the surface gleams, and the centering appears dead-on. You're confident it's a PSA 10... until PSA disagrees.

**The harsh reality:** Approximately 60-70% of submissions don't achieve PSA 10, and most failures are due to **hidden defects** invisible to the naked eye.

These subtle flaws‚Äîprint lines, silvering, micro-chipping, and more‚Äîcan cost you **$50-300+ in grading fees** for a card that will never reach your target grade. Even worse, the value differential between a PSA 10 and PSA 9 can be **3-5x** for premium cards.

In this comprehensive guide, you'll learn to identify the **10 most common hidden defects** that destroy PSA 10 grades, plus the exact inspection methodology professional graders use to spot them before submission.

### 1. Print Lines (Factory Defect) {#1-print-lines}

**What They Are:**  
Horizontal or vertical lines caused by the factory printing process. These appear as thin, consistent lines across the card surface, most visible in solid color areas or holographic patterns.

**Why They Destroy PSA 10:**  
PSA's standard: "A Gem Mint 10 is virtually perfect." While minor factory printing imperfections are allowed "if not visible from 16 inches away," print lines are almost always detectable under PSA's magnified inspection. Even faint print lines typically result in PSA 9 at best.

**How to Spot Them:**

**Tools Needed:**
- 10x jeweler's loupe ($10-20)
- Bright LED light (5000K recommended, $15-30)

**Detection Method:**
1. Hold the card at a 45-degree angle under bright light
2. Use 10x loupe to scan the entire surface
3. Look for thin lines in solid color backgrounds
4. Pay special attention to:
   - Border areas
   - Text boxes
   - Holographic patterns
   - Solid color regions
5. Rotate the card slowly‚Äîlines may only be visible at certain angles

**Common Locations:**
- Horizontal lines across the middle of the card (most common)
- Vertical lines through character artwork
- Lines in the card border or name plate

**Visual Example Notes:**

**Prevalence:** 40% of PSA 10 rejections  
**Fixable:** ‚ùå No (factory defect, cannot be repaired)  
**PSA Stance:** Almost always results in PSA 9 or lower

### 2. Silvering (Edge Whitening) {#2-silvering}

**What It Is:**  
Silvering is edge whitening caused by handling, storage, or manufacturing. It appears as white or light-colored areas along the card's edge where the colored ink layer has worn away or separated from the card stock.

**Why It Destroys PSA 10:**  
PSA 10 requires "immaculate edges, without chips or wear." Even minor silvering on a single edge prevents PSA 10. The strictness varies by card color (more noticeable on dark borders), but any visible edge whitening is grounds for downgrade.

**How to Spot It:**

**Tools Needed:**
- Bright natural light or 5000K LED
- Dark background (to contrast with white edges)
- Optional: 10x loupe for micro-silvering

**Detection Method:**
1. Place card on a dark surface (black cloth works well)
2. Examine all four edges under bright light
3. Hold card at eye level and look along each edge
4. Check BOTH front and back edges
5. Pay extra attention to:
   - Top and bottom edges (most common)
   - Corners (where two edges meet)
   - Back edges (PSA allows 75/25 centering on back but NO silvering)

**Front vs Back:**
- **Front silvering:** Automatic PSA 9 or lower
- **Back silvering:** Also prevents PSA 10 (common misconception that back is less strict)

**Common Causes:**
- Handling the card from packs (finger pressure on edges)
- Removing from sleeves (friction)
- Temperature/humidity changes (edge expansion)
- Poor storage (cards rubbing together)

**Visual Example Notes:**
*[Image would show: Card edge with and without silvering, under bright light. Close-up showing the difference between mint edge vs silvered edge]*

**Prevalence:** 30% of PSA 10 rejections  
**Fixable:** ‚ùå No (coloring edges is prohibited alteration)  
**PSA Stance:** Zero tolerance for visible silvering on PSA 10

### 3. Holo Scratches (Surface Damage) {#3-holo-scratches}

**What They Are:**  
Scratches on the holographic foil surface, ranging from light hairline scratches to deep gouges. These are most visible on holographic cards (Full Art, Reverse Holo, Rainbow Rare, etc.) but can occur on any card with a foil treatment.

**Why They Destroy PSA 10:**  
PSA requires "no visible defects or scratches on the surface" for Gem Mint 10. Holo scratches are immediately visible under PSA's inspection lighting and are one of the most common reasons for PSA 9 downgrades.

**How to Spot Them:**

**Tools Needed:**
- Bright LED light with adjustable angle
- 10x loupe
- Dark inspection room (to see light reflection better)

**Detection Method:**
1. Hold the card at various angles under bright light
2. Rotate slowly and watch for light catching scratches
3. Use the "spotlight technique":
   - Shine light at 30-45 degree angle
   - Move light source around the card
   - Scratches appear as light or dark lines depending on angle
4. Use 10x loupe to confirm and assess severity
5. Check the entire holo surface, not just the character

**Scratch Severity Levels:**

**Light Scratches:**
- Hairline thin
- Only visible at specific angles
- Typically result in PSA 9

**Moderate Scratches:**
- Visible at multiple angles
- May cross character artwork
- Result in PSA 8-9

**Heavy Scratches:**
- Visible without specific lighting
- Deep gouges in foil
- Result in PSA 7 or lower

**Common Locations:**
- Center of the card (character area)
- Holo borders
- Corners (from sleeve insertion/removal)

**Common Causes:**
- Sleeve insertion/removal
- Cards touching each other in storage
- Toploader friction
- Factory handling (yes, pack-fresh cards can have holo scratches)

**Visual Example Notes:**
*[Image would show: Holographic card under angled light showing hairline scratches. Comparison of light, moderate, and heavy scratch severity]*

**Prevalence:** 20% of PSA 10 rejections  
**Fixable:** ‚ùå No (surface damage cannot be repaired)  
**PSA Stance:** Even light scratches often prevent PSA 10

### 4. Soft Corners (Manufacturing Defect) {#4-soft-corners}

**What They Are:**  
Corners that appear rounded or "soft" rather than sharp and crisp. Unlike corner wear from handling (which shows whitening), soft corners are a manufacturing defect where the corner was never properly cut to a sharp 90-degree angle.

**Why They Destroy PSA 10:**  
PSA 10 requires "clean, sharp corners" (all four). Soft corners, even without any visible whitening or damage, fail this criterion. This defect is particularly frustrating because the card can be pack-fresh yet still ineligible for PSA 10.

**How to Spot Them:**

**Tools Needed:**
- 10x jeweler's loupe
- Bright light
- Reference card with known sharp corners (for comparison)

**Detection Method:**
1. Examine each corner under 10x magnification
2. Look for the corner point:
   - **Sharp corner:** Comes to a crisp point or near-point
   - **Soft corner:** Rounded, no defined point
3. Check all FOUR corners (even one soft corner prevents PSA 10)
4. Compare to a reference card if uncertain
5. View from multiple angles‚Äîsome soft corners only show from certain perspectives

**Soft Corners vs Corner Wear:**

| Feature | Soft Corner (Factory) | Corner Wear (Handling) |
|---------|----------------------|------------------------|
| **Appearance** | Rounded, smooth | May show whitening/fraying |
| **Cause** | Manufacturing | Handling damage |
| **Surface** | Clean, no whitening | Often shows edge whitening |
| **Fixable** | No | No |
| **PSA Result** | PSA 9 or lower | PSA 8 or lower (depending on severity) |

**Common Cards Affected:**
- Modern sets with quality control issues
- Certain manufacturers more prone to soft corners
- Cards from specific print runs

**Detection Tip:**  
Hold the card up to a bright background. Sharp corners will show a defined point silhouette; soft corners will show rounding.

**Visual Example Notes:**
*[Image would show: Side-by-side comparison of sharp corner vs soft corner under 10x magnification. Silhouette view showing the difference]*

**Prevalence:** ~5% of PSA 10 rejections (less common but highly frustrating)  
**Fixable:** ‚ùå No  
**PSA Stance:** Prevents PSA 10 even without any other defects

### 5. Centering Issues (Measurement Defect) {#5-centering-issues}

**What It Is:**  
Centering refers to how evenly the card's printed image is positioned within the card borders. PSA 10 has strict centering tolerances: **55/45 to 60/40 on the front, 75/25 on the back** (maximum).

**Why It Destroys PSA 10:**  
Centering outside these tolerances automatically disqualifies a card from PSA 10, even if perfect in every other way. This is a measurable, objective criterion.

**How to Spot It:**

**Tools Needed:**
- Ruler or digital calipers (precision recommended)
- Calculator
- Bright, even lighting

**Detection Method:**

**Step 1: Measure Borders**
1. Measure the left border width (in mm)
2. Measure the right border width
3. Measure the top border width
4. Measure the bottom border width

**Step 2: Calculate Ratios**
- **Left/Right ratio:** Divide smaller by larger, convert to percentage
  - Example: Left = 2mm, Right = 3mm ‚Üí 2/3 = 66.7% ‚Üí **67/33 ratio** ‚ùå (Fails, >60/40)
  - Example: Left = 2.5mm, Right = 2.8mm ‚Üí 2.5/2.8 = 89% ‚Üí **89/11 ratio** ‚úÖ But expressed as 55/45 ‚úÖ
  
- **Top/Bottom ratio:** Same calculation

**Step 3: Determine PSA Eligibility**
- **Front:** Both ratios must be 60/40 or better (55/45 ideal)
- **Back:** Both ratios must be 75/25 or better

**PSA Centering Tiers:**
- **PSA 10:** 55/45 to 60/40 front, 75/25 back (max)
- **PSA 9:** 65/35 to 70/30 front, 80/20 back (max)
- **PSA 8:** 70/30 to 75/25 front, 85/15 back (max)

**Quick Visual Test:**
Before measuring, do a quick visual check:
1. Look at border widths‚Äîobvious differences suggest poor centering
2. Compare opposite borders (left vs right, top vs bottom)
3. If borders look dramatically different, skip measurement and don't submit

**Common Centering Errors:**
- **Left/Right:** Most common centering issue
- **Top/Bottom:** Secondary concern (but still matters)
- **Back centering:** Often worse than front (PSA allows more tolerance)

**Visual Example Notes:**
*[Image would show: Centering measurement diagram with ruler showing 55/45 vs 65/35 examples. Visual guide to "good" vs "bad" centering at a glance]*

**Prevalence:** ~10% of PSA 10 rejections (but 100% predictable if you measure)  
**Fixable:** ‚ùå No  
**PSA Stance:** Hard cutoff‚Äîeven 61/39 front prevents PSA 10

### 6. Roller Marks (Factory Compression) {#6-roller-marks}

**What They Are:**  
Indentations or raised lines caused by the factory printing rollers compressing the card during production. These appear as subtle lines or patterns, often parallel, across the card surface.

**Why They Destroy PSA 10:**  
Roller marks are surface imperfections that fail PSA's "virtually perfect" surface requirement. While minor printing imperfections are sometimes allowed, roller marks are typically visible enough under magnification to warrant PSA 9.

**How to Spot Them:**

**Tools Needed:**
- 10x loupe
- Angled LED light (critical for detection)
- Dark background

**Detection Method:**
1. Use angled light (45 degrees) to create shadows
2. Slowly tilt the card under the light
3. Look for:
   - Parallel lines (from roller pattern)
   - Indented areas (looks slightly pressed)
   - Raised areas (embossed effect)
4. Feel with a gloved finger (optional)‚Äîyou may feel slight texture variation
5. Use 10x loupe to confirm visual findings

**Roller Marks vs Print Lines:**

| Feature | Roller Marks | Print Lines |
|---------|--------------|-------------|
| **Appearance** | Indented/raised texture | Flat surface lines |
| **Feel** | Slight texture (if severe) | Smooth |
| **Cause** | Compression during production | Ink printing |
| **Visibility** | Needs angled light | Visible in most lighting |

**Common Locations:**
- Across the entire card (parallel pattern)
- More visible on glossy or holo surfaces
- May appear as "waves" or subtle texture

**Common Card Types:**
- Prizm cards (notorious for roller marks)
- Topps Chrome
- Some Pokemon sets (varies by printer)

**Visual Example Notes:**
*[Image would show: Card under angled light showing roller mark indentations. Comparison of card with and without roller marks under same lighting]*

**Prevalence:** ~3-5% of PSA 10 rejections (varies by card type)  
**Fixable:** ‚ùå No  
**PSA Stance:** Treated as surface defect, typically prevents PSA 10

### 7. Surface Dents (Indentations) {#7-surface-dents}

**What They Are:**  
Small indentations in the card surface caused by pressure or impact. Unlike creases (which fold the card), dents are localized depressions that don't break the surface but create a visible dimple.

**Why They Destroy PSA 10:**  
Surface dents fail PSA's "no visible defects on the surface" criterion. Even tiny dents visible under magnification will result in PSA 9 or lower.

**How to Spot Them:**

**Tools Needed:**
- Angled LED light (essential)
- 10x loupe
- Dark inspection environment

**Detection Method:**
1. **Angle Lighting Technique** (most effective):
   - Hold card at 45-degree angle
   - Shine light across the surface (not directly down)
   - Slowly rotate the card
   - Dents will appear as dark spots or shadows
   
2. **Reflection Method** (for glossy cards):
   - Reflect light off the card surface
   - Dents will disrupt the reflection pattern
   
3. **Feel Test** (optional):
   - With gloved finger, lightly feel the surface
   - You may detect subtle depressions

4. Use 10x loupe to confirm size and severity

**Dent vs Crease vs Print Defect:**

| Defect Type | Appearance | Feel | Severity |
|-------------|------------|------|----------|
| **Dent** | Indentation, no surface break | Slight depression | PSA 8-9 |
| **Crease** | Fold line, surface disruption | Ridge/valley | PSA 6 or lower |
| **Print Defect** | Surface irregularity | Smooth | PSA 9 (if minor) |

**Common Causes:**
- Pressure from stacking (cards on top pressing down)
- Impact from dropping
- Fingernail pressure (during handling)
- Toploader insertion (edge pressure)
- Factory handling

**Common Locations:**
- Center of card (from stacking weight)
- Near edges (from toploader insertion)
- Corners (from impact)

**Visual Example Notes:**
*[Image would show: Card surface under angled light showing dent shadow. Close-up comparison of smooth surface vs dented surface]*

**Prevalence:** ~5% of PSA 10 rejections  
**Fixable:** ‚ùå No (pressing is prohibited)  
**PSA Stance:** Even minor dents prevent PSA 10

### 8. Edge Chipping (Micro-Damage) {#8-edge-chipping}

**What It Is:**  
Small chips or missing pieces along the card edge. These range from micro-chips (barely visible) to macro-chips (obvious damage). For PSA 10 consideration, we're focusing on **micro-chipping** that's hard to spot without magnification.

**Why It Destroys PSA 10:**  
PSA 10 requires "immaculate edges." Any chipping, no matter how small, violates this standard. Micro-chips are particularly frustrating because they're often invisible without proper inspection.

**How to Spot It:**

**Tools Needed:**
- 10x jeweler's loupe (essential)
- Bright light
- Dark background for contrast

**Detection Method:**
1. Place card on dark background
2. Examine each edge under 10x magnification
3. Look for:
   - Tiny missing pieces (looks like a "bite" out of the edge)
   - Irregular edge texture
   - White spots where the card stock is exposed
4. Check all FOUR edges completely (run the loupe along entire edge length)
5. Pay extra attention to corners (where two edges meet)

**Micro vs Macro Chipping:**

**Micro-Chipping:**
- Only visible under 10x magnification
- Pinpoint size (<0.5mm)
- Often mistaken for edge roughness
- Result: PSA 9

**Macro-Chipping:**
- Visible to naked eye
- 0.5mm+ size
- Obvious damage
- Result: PSA 8 or lower

**Edge Chipping vs Silvering:**

| Feature | Edge Chipping | Silvering |
|---------|---------------|-----------|
| **Appearance** | Missing piece, "bite" | Whitening, no missing piece |
| **Edge Surface** | Irregular, rough | Smooth but discolored |
| **Cause** | Impact, handling | Wear, separation |
| **Visibility** | 3D (depth) | 2D (color change) |

**Common Causes:**
- Removing from sleeves (edge catches)
- Toploader insertion (edge scrapes)
- Dropping cards
- Factory cutting imperfections
- Cards rubbing together in storage

**Common Locations:**
- Corners (most common)
- Top edge (from sleeve removal)
- Bottom edge (from toploader insertion)

**Visual Example Notes:**
*[Image would show: Card edge under 10x magnification showing micro-chip vs clean edge. Diagram showing difference between chipping and silvering]*

**Prevalence:** ~5-7% of PSA 10 rejections  
**Fixable:** ‚ùå No  
**PSA Stance:** Zero tolerance for visible chipping on PSA 10

### 9. Color Fading/Bleeding (Print Quality) {#9-color-fading}

**What It Is:**  
Color fading is when the ink appears washed out or lighter than intended. Color bleeding is when ink spreads beyond its intended boundaries, creating blurred or overlapping colors. Both are print quality defects.

**Why It Destroys PSA 10:**  
PSA 10 requires consistent, vibrant colors and sharp print quality. Fading or bleeding indicates print quality issues that fail the "virtually perfect" standard.

**How to Spot It:**

**Tools Needed:**
- Reference card (same card, known good print)
- Bright natural light (5000K LED recommended)
- 10x loupe (for bleeding inspection)

**Detection Method:**

**For Color Fading:**
1. Compare your card to a reference (image online, another copy, or pack-fresh example)
2. Look for:
   - Washed-out colors (less vibrant)
   - Lighter ink density
   - Inconsistent color saturation
3. Check under bright, neutral light (not yellow/warm lighting)

**For Color Bleeding:**
1. Use 10x loupe to examine:
   - Text edges (should be crisp)
   - Color boundaries (where two colors meet)
   - Fine details (Pokemon features, card border)
2. Look for:
   - Fuzzy text
   - Colors overlapping
   - "Halo" effect around text or images

**Fading vs Bleeding vs Normal Variation:**

| Defect | Sign | Cause | PSA Impact |
|--------|------|-------|------------|
| **Fading** | Washed-out colors | UV exposure, storage, or print quality | PSA 8-9 |
| **Bleeding** | Fuzzy edges, color overlap | Print quality | PSA 9 |
| **Normal Variation** | Slight color differences between prints | Factory batch differences | May still achieve PSA 10 if within tolerance |

**Common Causes:**

**Fading:**
- UV exposure (sunlight, fluorescent lights)
- Chemical exposure (PVC from bad sleeves)
- Poor storage conditions
- Low-quality print run

**Bleeding:**
- Print misalignment
- Excess ink during printing
- Low-quality printing equipment
- Factory defect

**Common Locations:**
- **Fading:** Entire card or sun-exposed areas
- **Bleeding:** Text, fine lines, color borders

**Visual Example Notes:**
*[Image would show: Side-by-side comparison of faded vs normal color saturation. Close-up of color bleeding around text vs crisp text]*

**Prevalence:** ~2-3% of PSA 10 rejections (less common)  
**Fixable:** ‚ùå No (re-coloring is prohibited alteration)  
**PSA Stance:** Noticeable fading or bleeding prevents PSA 10

### 10. PVC Damage (Storage Defect) {#10-pvc-damage}

**What It Is:**  
Chemical damage caused by polyvinyl chloride (PVC) in cheap card sleeves and binders. PVC breaks down over time and releases chemicals that bond to the card surface, creating a sticky, hazy, or stained appearance.

**Why It Destroys PSA 10:**  
PVC damage creates visible surface defects (haze, stickiness, stains) that fail PSA's "free from scratches, print lines, fading, or imperfections" surface requirement. In severe cases, PSA may refuse to grade the card at all.

**How to Spot It:**

**Tools Needed:**
- Black light (UV light, $10-20)
- Bright light
- Clean reference card for comparison
- Optional: 10x loupe

**Detection Method:**

**Visual Inspection:**
1. Hold card under bright light
2. Look for:
   - Hazy or cloudy surface (like a film)
   - Sticky or tacky feel
   - Discoloration or yellowing
   - Rainbow-like sheen (from chemical residue)

**Black Light Test:**
1. Shine UV black light on the card surface
2. PVC damage will often fluoresce or show uneven reflection
3. Compare to a known clean card

**Feel Test:**
1. With clean, gloved hands, lightly touch the surface
2. PVC-damaged cards feel sticky or tacky
3. Clean cards feel smooth and slick

**PVC Damage vs Other Defects:**

| Defect | Appearance | Feel | Cause | Reversible? |
|--------|------------|------|-------|-------------|
| **PVC Damage** | Hazy, cloudy, sticky | Tacky | Chemical bonding from bad sleeves | Sometimes (professional restoration) |
| **Holo Scratches** | Linear marks | Smooth | Physical damage | No |
| **Print Defect** | Lines or irregularities | Smooth | Factory | No |

**Common Causes:**
- Storing cards in PVC sleeves (common in old binders)
- Ultra Pro "penny sleeves" (older versions)
- Binder pages made with PVC
- Long-term storage in non-archival materials

**Prevention:**
- Use polypropylene (PP) sleeves (safe alternative)
- Look for "PVC-free" or "archival safe" labeling
- Replace old binders/sleeves
- Store in climate-controlled environment

**Common Locations:**
- Entire surface (PVC affects the whole card)
- More visible on dark or holo surfaces

**Visual Example Notes:**
*[Image would show: Card with PVC haze under normal light and UV light. Comparison of PVC-damaged vs clean surface]*

**Prevalence:** ~1-2% of PSA 10 rejections (less common in modern storage practices)  
**Fixable:** ‚ö†Ô∏è Sometimes (professional restoration, but risky and may be detected by PSA)  
**PSA Stance:** Moderate to severe PVC damage prevents grading; minor damage results in PSA 8 or lower

## The 6-Step Inspection Checklist {#inspection-checklist}

Now that you know what to look for, here's the exact methodology to inspect your cards before PSA submission:

### Step 1: Environment Setup (2 minutes)

**Tools Needed:**
- ‚úÖ 10x jeweler's loupe ($10-20 on Amazon)
- ‚úÖ 5000K LED light source ($15-30)
- ‚úÖ Dark background cloth (black microfiber)
- ‚úÖ Cotton gloves ($5)
- ‚úÖ Ruler or digital calipers (for centering)
- ‚úÖ Optional: UV black light ($15)

**Setup:**
1. Clean, flat surface
2. Bright, neutral lighting (5000K "daylight" LED)
3. Dark background for contrast
4. Put on cotton gloves to avoid fingerprints/oils

**Time:** 2 minutes

---

### Step 2: Naked Eye Inspection (3 minutes)

**Without magnification, check:**

1. **Centering:**
   - Visual check: Are borders obviously uneven?
   - If yes, measure with ruler (see Defect #5)
   - Reject if: Front >60/40 or Back >75/25

2. **Overall Condition:**
   - Obvious creases, dents, or damage? ‚Üí Don't submit
   - Color fading or bleeding? ‚Üí Compare to reference

3. **Corner Sharpness:**
   - All four corners look sharp?
   - Any visible rounding or whitening?

4. **Edge Integrity:**
   - Any visible chipping or silvering?
   - Hold at eye level and look along each edge

**If any major defects found, STOP. Don't submit.**

**Time:** 3 minutes

---

### Step 3: Magnified Inspection (5 minutes)

**With 10x loupe, check:**

1. **Print Lines (Defect #1):**
   - Hold at 45-degree angle under light
   - Scan entire surface
   - Look in borders, text boxes, solid colors

2. **Holo Scratches (Defect #3):**
   - Angle light across holo surface
   - Rotate slowly, watch for light catching scratches
   - Check character area and borders

3. **Soft Corners (Defect #4):**
   - Examine all four corners under 10x
   - Sharp point or rounded?
   - Compare to reference card if uncertain

4. **Edge Chipping (Defect #8):**
   - Run loupe along all four edges
   - Look for tiny "bites" or missing pieces
   - Check corners carefully (where edges meet)

**Time:** 5 minutes

---

### Step 4: Angle Lighting Test (2 minutes)

**Tilt card under angled light (45 degrees):**

1. **Silvering (Defect #2):**
   - Hold card at eye level
   - Look along each edge
   - White/light areas visible? ‚Üí Silvering present

2. **Surface Dents (Defect #7):**
   - Slowly rotate card under angled light
   - Look for shadows or dark spots (indicating dents)
   - Confirm with loupe if spotted

3. **Roller Marks (Defect #6):**
   - Tilt card slowly
   - Look for parallel indented lines
   - May feel slight texture with gloved finger

**Time:** 2 minutes

---

### Step 5: Black Light Test - Optional (2 minutes)

**If you have UV black light:**

1. **PVC Damage (Defect #10):**
   - Shine UV light on card surface
   - Look for uneven fluorescence or chemical residue
   - Compare to known clean card

2. **UV Exposure Damage:**
   - Faded areas may show differently under UV
   - Helps detect sun damage

3. **Restoration Detection:**
   - Re-colored edges or surface alterations may fluoresce

**Time:** 2 minutes (if using black light)

---

### Step 6: Documentation & Decision (1 minute)

**Record your findings:**

1. **Photo any defects** (for reference, or to consult with experts)

2. **Grade Prediction:**
   - No defects found ‚Üí **Submit for PSA 10**
   - Minor defect (1-2 light issues) ‚Üí **Submit for PSA 9** (if card value justifies)

## Frequently Asked Questions {#faq}

### 1. What are the most common hidden defects in PSA grading?

The most common hidden defects that prevent PSA 10 grades are:
1. **Print lines** (40% of rejections) - Factory defects visible under magnification
2. **Silvering** (30%) - Edge whitening from handling or storage
3. **Holo scratches** (20%) - Surface scratches on holographic foil
4. **Soft corners** (5-10%) - Rounded corners from manufacturing

These defects are "hidden" because they require magnification (10x loupe), specific lighting angles, or proper tools to detect. A card can appear perfect to the naked eye yet fail PSA 10 due to these subtle flaws.

---

### 2. How can I spot print lines on Pokemon cards?

To spot print lines:
1. **Use a 10x jeweler's loupe** ($10-20 investment)
2. **Hold the card at a 45-degree angle** under bright LED light (5000K recommended)
3. **Scan the entire surface**, paying attention to solid color areas, borders, and text boxes
4. **Look for thin, consistent lines** (horizontal or vertical) across the surface
5. **Rotate the card slowly** - print lines may only be visible at certain angles

**Common locations:** Card borders, holographic patterns, solid background colors. Print lines are factory defects and account for approximately 40% of PSA 10 rejections.

---

### 3. What is silvering in card grading?

Silvering is **edge whitening** caused by handling, storage, or manufacturing. It appears as white or light-colored areas along the card edge where the colored ink layer has worn away or separated from the card stock.

**How to detect:**
1. Place card on a dark background
2. Examine all four edges under bright light
3. Hold the card at eye level and look along each edge
4. Check both front and back edges

**PSA Stance:** Zero tolerance for visible silvering on PSA 10. Even minor edge whitening on a single edge will prevent Gem Mint 10 grade. Silvering accounts for approximately 30% of PSA 10 rejections.

---

### 4. Can I fix card defects before PSA submission?

**No.** PSA prohibits any alteration or restoration, including:
- ‚ùå Pressing cards to remove dents
- ‚ùå Coloring edges to hide silvering
- ‚ùå Trimming edges
- ‚ùå Cleaning with chemicals
- ‚ùå Any surface alterations

**Attempting to fix defects will result in:**
- Card rejection
- Loss of grading fees
- Potential ban from PSA services
- "Altered" designation on the card

**If your card has defects:** Submit with accurate grade expectations, hold the card, or use professional pre-grade evaluation to determine if submission is worthwhile.

---

### 5. What tools do I need to inspect cards for grading?

**Essential Tools ($30-70 total):**

1. **10x Jeweler's Loupe** ($10-20)
   - Detects print lines, holo scratches, soft corners, edge chipping

2. **5000K LED Light Source** ($15-30)
   - Provides neutral "daylight" lighting for accurate inspection

3. **Cotton Gloves** ($5)
   - Prevents fingerprints and oils during handling

4. **Ruler or Digital Calipers** ($5-15)
   - Measures centering (55/45 front, 75/25 back tolerance)

**Optional Tools:**
5. **UV Black Light** ($10-20)

6. **Dark Background Cloth** ($5)
   - Black microfiber for contrast when checking edges

**ROI:** These tools pay for themselves after preventing one bad submission ($50-300 grading fee saved).

---

### 6. What's the difference between soft corners and hard corners?

| Feature | Soft Corners | Hard Corners |
|---------|--------------|--------------|
| **Appearance** | Rounded, no sharp point | Crisp, sharp 90-degree angle |
| **Cause** | Manufacturing defect | Proper factory cut |
| **Visibility** | Requires 10x loupe | Visible to naked eye (when sharp) |
| **Whitening** | Usually none (no damage) | No whitening |
| **PSA Result** | PSA 9 or lower | PSA 10 eligible (if no other defects) |

**How to check:**
1. Examine each corner under 10x magnification
2. Look for a sharp point vs rounded "softness"
3. Compare to a reference card with known sharp corners
4. All four corners must be sharp for PSA 10

**Frustration factor:** High - Card can be pack-fresh yet have soft corners preventing PSA 10.

---

### 7. How strict is PSA on factory defects vs handling damage?

**PSA treats factory defects and handling damage equally for PSA 10 consideration.**

PSA's official stance: *"A Gem Mint 10 is virtually perfect. An allowance can be made for a slight printing imperfection, so long as it doesn't impair the overall appeal of the card."*

**In practice:**
- **Minor factory defects** (very faint print lines not visible from 16 inches) *may* be allowed, but this is rare
- **Moderate factory defects** (visible print lines, roller marks, soft corners) ‚Üí PSA 9 or lower
- **Handling damage** (holo scratches, silvering, edge chipping) ‚Üí PSA 9 or lower

**Bottom line:** Don't assume factory defects get special treatment. If the defect is visible under PSA's magnified inspection, it will likely prevent PSA 10, regardless of cause.

---

### 8. Should I submit my card if I find a hidden defect?

**It depends on:**

**1. Defect Severity:**
- **Very minor** (light print line, one slightly soft corner) ‚Üí Submit for PSA 9 (if value justifies)
- **Moderate** (visible silvering, multiple defects) ‚Üí Hold
- **Severe** (heavy scratches, poor centering) ‚Üí Don't submit

**2. Card Value:**
- **PSA 9 value > $100:** Consider submitting (authentication + grade value)
- **PSA 9 value < $100:** Likely not worth grading fee ($50-150)

**3. Personal Goals:**
- **Investment:** Only submit if PSA 9+ value justifies cost
- **Personal collection:** Grade for authentication and preservation

**Decision Matrix:**
- Zero defects + high value ‚Üí **Submit for PSA 10**
- One minor defect + medium value ‚Üí **Submit for PSA 9**
- Multiple defects or low value ‚Üí **Hold**
- Uncertain about severity ‚Üí **Get professional pre-grade evaluation**

---

### 9. What is centering tolerance for PSA 10?

**PSA 10 Centering Requirements:**
- **Front:** 55/45 to 60/40 (maximum)
- **Back:** 75/25 (maximum)

**How to measure:**
1. Measure border widths on opposite sides (left vs right, top vs bottom)
2. Calculate the ratio: smaller √∑ larger
3. Convert to percentage

**Example:**
- Left border: 2.5mm, Right border: 3mm
- Ratio: 2.5 √∑ 3 = 0.833 = 83.3%
- Expressed as: 83/17 or approximately **55/45** ‚úÖ (Passes)

**Example 2 (Fails):**
- Left border: 2mm, Right border: 3.5mm
- Ratio: 2 √∑ 3.5 = 0.571 = 57.1%
- Expressed as: 57/43 or approximately **60/40** ‚úÖ (Barely passes)
- If it were 2mm vs 4mm ‚Üí 50/50 expressed as **67/33** ‚ùå (Fails)

**Both left/right AND top/bottom must meet tolerance.**

**PSA Grading Tiers:**
- PSA 10: 60/40 max (front), 75/25 max (back)
- PSA 9: 70/30 max (front), 90/10 max (back)
- PSA 8: 75/25 max (front), 90/10 max (back)

---

### 10. Can hidden defects be seen in regular photos?

**No.** Hidden defects cannot be reliably detected in regular photos because they require:

1. **Magnification** (10x loupe) - Photos lack this detail
2. **Specific lighting angles** (45-degree angled light) - Photos use flat lighting
3. **Physical inspection** (feeling for dents, rotating for holo scratches) - Photos are static

**What photos MISS:**
- ‚ùå Print lines (too subtle for camera)
- ‚ùå Micro-chipping (too small to photograph)
- ‚ùå Soft corners (need magnification to distinguish from sharp)
- ‚ùå Light silvering (camera flash washes it out)
- ‚ùå Surface dents (need angled light to create shadows)

**This is why in-person inspection with proper tools is critical before submission.**

**Exception:** High-resolution macro photography with professional lighting *may* capture some defects, but still misses many that PSA's in-person inspection will catch.
